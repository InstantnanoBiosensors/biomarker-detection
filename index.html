The Problem

Why Sensitivity Alone Is Not Enough

Many clinically important biomarkers require femtomolar or attomolar detection. But detecting a small signal is only meaningful when it originates from the intended target. Greater sensitivity can reveal the correct biomarker, or magnify signals caused by background, cross-reactivity, and poor antibody-pair selection.

Failure mode 1The “Needle in a Haystack” Problem

The target is difficult to distinguish from background.

Low target abundance, nonspecific binding, blank variation, and matrix interference can obscure the true signal even when the instrument is highly sensitive.

Failure mode 2The “Lookalike” Problem

The assay must distinguish the intended molecular form.

Isoforms, fragments, unmodified proteins, and related post-translational modifications may produce misleading signals when antibody specificity is insufficient.

Failure mode 3The “Spatial” Problem

Strong antibodies do not always form a strong pair.

Antibodies may compete for overlapping regions, create steric interference, dissociate too quickly, or perform differently when their capture and detector roles are reversed.

Failure mode 4The “Reproducibility” Problem

Antibody performance can change across batches and conditions.

Binding behavior may vary with antibody batch, buffer composition, reagent formulation, storage, handling, and sample environment.

A low LoD has little value if the assay is measuring the wrong target.

The Solution

From Selection to Detection

Make the antibody decisions before scaling sensitivity

FOPPR characterizes, selects, and qualifies antibody candidates before transfer to FONLISA or another assay platform. Unsuitable candidates are removed earlier, reducing downstream optimization and development risk.

1
Characterize Binding and Target Specificity

Measure association, dissociation, and affinity: ka, kd, and KD.

Compare binding against selected potential cross-reactants to assess target recognition and specificity.

2
Select a Compatible Antibody Pair

Use epitope screening to identify candidates that can bind simultaneously under the tested conditions. Remove combinations showing competition or steric interference and prioritize compatible pairs for downstream development.

FOPPR can screen up to four antibodies sequentially on the same antigen-coated sensor in one uninterrupted run, without regeneration between injections.

See the four-antibody workflow
3
Qualify and Monitor Antibody Performance

Compare antibody batches and evaluate binding under different buffers, formulations, storage conditions, and sample environments before transfer and during ongoing quality monitoring.

4
Transfer and Optimize Detection

Transfer the selected pair to FONLISA, Simoa, or another assay platform. Optimize the platform-specific format, then confirm blank variability, recovery, precision, dilution behavior, parallelism, and matrix interference.

FOPPR can continue to support antibody batch to batch variation and reagent-condition monitoring after assay development.

Distinctive Capability

Up to Four Sequential Antibody Challenges on One Sensor

FOPPR introduces up to four antibodies sequentially to the same immobilized antigen population during one uninterrupted experiment. Each signal change shows whether the newly introduced antibody can bind to the progressively occupied surface or is blocked by epitope overlap or steric interference.

Important distinction

This is sequential cumulative screening, not simultaneous multiplexing. Alternative injection orders may be needed to identify which earlier antibody caused a blocked response and complete the epitope map.

Conventional Pairwise Evaluation

Conventional methods commonly test ordered antibody pairs through separate assay cycles or a pairwise matrix.

Case Study 1

When a Strong Signal May Be the Wrong Signal

FOPPR → Simoa

This study illustrates why antibody specificity should be resolved before an assay is pushed toward lower detection limits.

Clinical need
Lewy body disorders can be difficult to distinguish from Alzheimer’s disease due to overlapping clinical features. Although the target protein is a promising neuroinflammatory biomarker candidate, a previous Ella-based assay had an LLoD of 52.9 pg/mL, leaving multiple samples undetectable, and relied on polyclonal antibodies prone to batch variability and cross-reactivity.
FOPPR finding
Real-time kinetic analysis, sequential epitope screening, and tests against selected related proteins identified candidates with compatible binding behavior. Several alternatives reportedly produced greater responses to non-target homolog than to the target protein under the tested conditions.
Selection and transfer
FOPPR data supported selection of the best antibody pair based on target binding, epitope compatibility, and low observed cross-reactivity. The pair was transferred to Simoa, where platform-specific optimization reportedly produced an LLoD of 0.618 pg/mL, approximately 86-fold lower than the previous assay.
Takeaway
FOPPR did not directly create the lower Simoa LLoD. It reduced the risk of optimizing an ultra-sensitive assay around antibodies with unsuitable specificity or pair compatibility.

Case Study 2

pTau217: A PTM-Defined Target at Sub-Femtogram Levels

FOPPR → FONLISA

pTau217 presents a different problem. The target is not simply tau at a low concentration; it is tau carrying a specific phosphorylation event at threonine 217. The antibody system must therefore distinguish the intended molecular form while retaining enough sensitivity for plasma measurement.

Broader Applicability

External Evaluation of FONLISA for Plasma NfL

FOPPR and FONLISA are not limited to a single biomarker, antibody supplier, or preconfigured assay. The workflow can be adapted to different antibody pairs and biomarker targets with application-specific qualification and optimization. As an example of this broader applicability, Madison Honey, a PhD candidate at Amsterdam University Medical Centre, discusses a prototype FONLISA assay for measuring plasma neurofilament light chain. In the reported study, FONLISA differentiated control samples from samples from patients with Alzheimer’s disease and showed strong agreement with a single-molecule array assay, supporting further investigation of the portable platform for rapid biomarker testing.
